Navigating the cold chain Landscape

Post-2020, there's been a significant evolution. We've seen a surge in demand for cold storage driven by various factors. For instance, the North American Cold Chain Market, including Canada, is growing rapidly. It was valued at USD 81.2 billion in 2022 and is expected to reach USD 142.6 billion by 2028. This growth is attributed to several forces, including:

  • E-commerce: The Canadian e-commerce boom, with retail sales skyrocketing from 3.9% in 2019 to 6.2% in 2022, has triggered a need for robust cold chain logistics to ensure the delivery of perishable online orders.
  • Shifting Consumer Preferences: A notable shift in consumer behavior towards frozen and perishable foods, particularly during and after the pandemic, has necessitated an expansion in cold chain capacity and technological advancements to cater to these evolving demands.
  • Export Boom: Canada's thriving export market for agricultural, fishery, and intermediate food products, exemplified by the 11.9% growth in January 2022 exports, underscores the crucial role the cold chain plays in facilitating international trade

Other than that, Technological Innovations like real-time temperature monitoring guarantee product quality and compliance, while data analytics facilitate operational efficiency through demand forecasting and route optimization is fueling the market. Additionally, IoT and RFID tags provide transparency with real-time shipment tracking, revolutionizing the industry with enhanced control and visibility

Canada's post-pandemic surge in perishable demand propels the Cold Chain industry to a projected $142.6 billion valuation by 2028, driven by tech and green practices.

Market Dynamic and Growth

The Cold Chain market is experiencing an unprecedented boom, with key drivers being a significant reason in consumer demand for frozen and perishable goods, technological advancements, and a heightened focus on sustainability.

Consumer habit is making a difference.

Since the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, consumer habits have dramatically shifted, with a marked preference for frozen and perishable products. This trend has necessitated an expansion of the cold chain infrastructure to maintain the integrity and supply of these goods. In North America, these trends are quantified by a projected Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) of 9.7% from 2022 to 2028, indicative of a robust and sustained market expansion.

Responding to this surge, the market has undergone considerable growth in cold storage capacities and the refinement of logistics solutions. The surge in the construction of new, advanced facilities has been noteworthy. At the same time, there's been a significant adoption of Internet of Things (IoT) technologies, which are pivotal for enhanced inventory management and operational oversight.

Canada's Cold Chain market's valuation is expected to grow from USD 81.2 billion in 2022 to an anticipated USD 142.6 billion by 2028. This substantial increase is a mirror to the growth and dynamism within the Canadian market, reflecting country's expanding operational capacities and increasing technological investments.

Expanding to Rural & Northern Areas

The expansion of cold chain logistics into Canada's rural and northern territories is a direct response to increased online shopping, with 55% of Canadians making mobile retail purchases as of January 2022. This digital trend is particularly impactful in remote areas, where access to physical stores is limited.

However, servicing these regions presents unique challenges due to their isolation and extreme weather, which complicate transportation and cold storage, compounded by the high costs and logistical difficulties of providing necessary high-speed internet connectivity.

The surge in e-commerce has significantly influenced cold chain strategies. The growth in retail e-commerce sales from 3.9% in 2019 to 6.2% in 2022 indicates a critical need for specialized logistics solutions. To address these challenges, technological advancements, such as the use of telematics in refrigerated transport, are being employed to maintain perishable goods' integrity across the long transit routes typical of these areas.

Overall, the adoption of advanced technologies is key to navigating the logistical hurdles and meeting the rising demand for cold chain services in Canada's less accessible regions, ensuring that even the most remote communities have access to fresh goods through robust, efficient cold chain logistics networks.
